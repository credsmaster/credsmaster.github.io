# The Book of Acts
---

## Chapter 1
---

### 1.1 · The Promise of the Holy Spirit (1:1–11)

**Key verse:** _"But you will receive power when the Holy Spirit comes on you; and you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth." — Acts 1:8_

#### Facts
- Luke addresses his second volume to Theophilus, summarising his first (the Gospel) and picking up immediately after the resurrection.
- Jesus appears to the apostles over forty days, speaking about the kingdom of God, before commanding them to wait in Jerusalem for the promised Holy Spirit.
- Two angels appear as Jesus ascends, assuring the apostles he will return in the same way he left.

#### Insights
- Acts 1:8 is the structural backbone of the entire book — Jerusalem (chs. 1–7), Judea and Samaria (chs. 8–12), and the ends of the earth (chs. 13–28). The mission is geographic and expanding.
- The disciples ask about restoring the kingdom to Israel (v.6); Jesus redirects from political timelines to Spirit-empowered witness. The question is not wrong — just premature and too small.
- The ascension is not abandonment; it is the condition for Pentecost. Jesus must go so the Spirit can come (cf. John 16:7).

#### Deeds
- Ask yourself: Am I waiting on God's timing and empowering before rushing into ministry, or do I act in my own strength?
- Reflect on Acts 1:8 as a personal commission — where is your "Jerusalem" today, and have you reached it before looking to the ends of the earth?

### 1.2 · Matthias Chosen to Replace Judas (1:12–26)

**Key verse:** _"Lord, you know everyone's heart. Show us which of these two you have chosen." — Acts 1:24_

#### Facts
- The apostles return to Jerusalem and gather in an upper room — about 120 believers in total, including the women and Jesus' family.
- Peter addresses the group, citing Psalms 69 and 109 to explain Judas's fate and the need to replace him.
- Two men are nominated — Joseph Barsabbas and Matthias. The group prays and casts lots; Matthias is chosen.

#### Insights
- The 120 praying together before Pentecost is a picture of the church in its simplest form: united, expectant, and dependent on God.
- Peter's handling of Judas's betrayal as Scripture fulfillment shows early Christian hermeneutics — crisis is read through the lens of God's sovereign plan, not just tragedy.
- The casting of lots is not superstition here but a recognised Old Testament practice of discerning God's will. After Pentecost, lots are never used again — the Spirit becomes the guide.

#### Deeds
- In times of loss or betrayal in community, do you look for God's hand in Scripture, or only in circumstances?
- Consider the discipline of communal prayer before major decisions. Is that a pattern in your church or household?
