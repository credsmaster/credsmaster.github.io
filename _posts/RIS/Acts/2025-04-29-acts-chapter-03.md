# The Book of Acts
---

## Chapter 3
---

### 3.1 · Peter Heals the Lame Beggar (3:1–10)

**Key verse:** _"Silver or gold I do not have, but what I do have I give you. In the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth, walk." — Acts 3:6_

#### Facts
- Peter and John go to the temple at the hour of prayer (3 p.m.) and encounter a man lame from birth, carried daily to beg at the Beautiful Gate.
- Peter fixes his eyes on the man, declares he has no silver or gold, and heals him in Jesus's name.
- The man walks, leaps, and enters the temple courts praising God — to the amazement of all who recognise him.

#### Insights
- The healing at the Beautiful Gate is a sign, not just a miracle — it points to the restoration that Jesus brings. The man lame from birth represents humanity barred from full access to God, now made whole and entering the temple freely.
- Peter's words — "what I do have I give you" — are a model of ministry. The church's greatest gift to the world is not wealth or influence but the name of Jesus.
- The man's response (walking, jumping, praising) is not restrained. Genuine encounter with Christ tends to overflow — not quietly seep.

#### Deeds
- What do you actually have to give in ministry? Take stock this week — not of what you lack, but of what Christ has placed in your hands.
- Reflect on the last time you were moved to public, uninhibited praise. What prompted it? What holds you back?

### 3.2 · Peter's Second Sermon (3:11–26)

**Key verse:** _"Repent, then, and turn to God, so that your sins may be wiped out, that times of refreshing may come from the Lord." — Acts 3:19_

#### Facts
- The healed man clings to Peter and John in Solomon's Colonnade; a crowd gathers, and Peter preaches.
- Peter deflects all glory from himself and John, attributing the miracle to Jesus whom they killed and God raised.
- He calls the crowd to repentance and identifies Jesus as the promised prophet like Moses (Deut. 18:15) and the fulfilment of all the covenants from Abraham onwards.

#### Insights
- Peter's repeated pattern of "you killed him — God raised him" is bold, not tactless. It is the gospel in its sharpest form: human responsibility and divine rescue held together.
- "Times of refreshing" (v.19) is a stunning promise — repentance is not merely a gate of pardon, but the door to ongoing renewal and the presence of God.
- Peter frames the gospel as the completion of Israel's own story. He does not call Jews to abandon their heritage but to see it fulfilled. This is an important model for contextual witness.

#### Deeds
- How does your understanding of repentance shape how you experience your walk with God — as guilt management, or as ongoing refreshing?
- Who in your life might respond to the gospel if it were framed as the fulfilment of what they already believe or hope for?
