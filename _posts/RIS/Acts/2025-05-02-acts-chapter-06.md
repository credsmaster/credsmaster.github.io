# The Book of Acts
---

## Chapter 6
---

### 6.1 · The Choosing of the Seven (6:1–7)

**Key verse:** _"It would not be right for us to neglect the ministry of the word of God in order to wait on tables." — Acts 6:2_

#### Facts
- As the church grows, Hellenistic Jews complain that their widows are being overlooked in the daily food distribution compared to Hebraic Jews.
- The Twelve address the congregation and propose appointing seven men of good reputation, full of the Spirit and wisdom, to oversee this service.
- Seven men are chosen — all with Greek names, notably including Nicolas, a proselyte from Antioch — and are presented to the apostles who pray and lay hands on them.
- The word of God spreads, the number of disciples increases greatly, and a large number of priests become obedient to the faith.

#### Insights
- The first recorded church conflict is not theological but logistical — and it falls along ethnic lines. The church's response is structural wisdom, not blame. Problems in growth require new forms of organisation.
- The qualifications for this practical role — full of the Spirit and wisdom — are the same as for preaching. There is no two-tier spirituality in the early church; all service is sacred.
- Choosing seven men with Greek names to serve the Greek-speaking widows is a culturally sensitive solution. The church does not just acknowledge the grievance — it addresses it with people who understand the culture.

#### Deeds
- When conflict arises in your community, do you look for a structural solution or just try to smooth over feelings?
- Reflect on how you value "practical" service roles. Are they filled by Spirit-filled, wise people in your context?

### 6.2 · Stephen Arrested (6:8–15)

**Key verse:** _"They could not stand up against the wisdom the Spirit gave him as he spoke." — Acts 6:10_

#### Facts
- Stephen, full of grace and power, performs signs and wonders among the people.
- Members of various synagogues dispute with him but cannot refute him; they secretly incite men to accuse him of speaking against Moses and God.
- Stephen is brought before the Sanhedrin; false witnesses claim he spoke against the temple and the law.
- As he stands before the council, his face appears like the face of an angel.

#### Insights
- Stephen is introduced first as a table-server (ch. 6), then as a preacher and miracle-worker. The Spirit does not confine himself to formal roles — he equips the willing.
- The opposition to Stephen follows a pattern: when argument fails, false accusation steps in. This is the same strategy used against Jesus. Authentic gospel witness tends to provoke the same response across every generation.
- Stephen's angelic face before the Sanhedrin is a detail of witness — even his opponents see something they cannot fully explain. The Spirit's presence can be visible even before a word is spoken.

#### Deeds
- Are you growing in the kind of Spirit-given wisdom that cannot be easily refuted? This is not cleverness — it comes from depth of life in God.
- How do you respond when opposition to your faith shifts from argument to personal attack? Note Stephen's composure here.
