# The Book of Acts
---

## Chapter 10
---

### 10.1 · Cornelius Calls for Peter (10:1–23a)

**Key verse:** _"Your prayers and gifts to the poor have come up as a memorial offering before God." — Acts 10:4_

#### Facts
- Cornelius, a Roman centurion in Caesarea, is described as devout, God-fearing, generous to the poor, and prayerful.
- An angel appears to him and directs him to send for Simon Peter, who is staying with Simon the tanner in Joppa.
- Meanwhile, Peter receives a vision of a sheet descending from heaven, filled with unclean animals. A voice commands him to kill and eat; he refuses three times. The voice says: "Do not call anything impure that God has made clean."
- The vision is repeated three times. As Peter is puzzling over it, Cornelius's men arrive.

#### Insights
- Cornelius is not a casual inquirer — he is a seeker with an established pattern of prayer and generosity. God responds to persistent seeking, even outside the covenant community. This anticipates the full inclusion of the Gentiles.
- Peter's triple vision follows the triple question Jesus put to him after the resurrection (John 21): both require him to overcome deep resistance. The Spirit works patiently with our prejudices.
- The divine timing is precise: Peter's vision ends exactly as Cornelius's messengers arrive (v.17–18). God orchestrates both sides of the story simultaneously. Neither party could have arranged this meeting.

#### Deeds
- Are there categories of people you have — even unconsciously — written off as unlikely recipients of the gospel? Ask God to give you a "sheet vision" about one such group.
- Cornelius's prayers and generosity were noticed by God before the gospel reached him. What you do in the waiting period of faith matters.

### 10.2 · Peter at Cornelius's House (10:23b–48)

**Key verse:** _"I now realise how true it is that God does not show favouritism but accepts from every nation the one who fears him and does what is right." — Acts 10:34–35_

#### Facts
- Peter travels to Caesarea and finds Cornelius, his family, and close friends gathered and waiting.
- Peter declares that God has shown him to call no person impure or unclean, and asks why he was summoned.
- Cornelius recounts his angelic visit; Peter preaches the gospel — Jesus's life, death, resurrection, and appointment as judge of the living and the dead.
- While Peter is still speaking, the Holy Spirit falls on all who hear the message, and they speak in tongues and praise God.
- The Jewish believers who came with Peter are astonished; Peter orders Cornelius's household to be baptised.

#### Insights
- Peter's sermon to Cornelius (vv. 34–43) is a concise summary of the gospel's biographical core: anointing, ministry of healing, crucifixion, resurrection, appearances, and commission to proclaim. This is the kerygma — the essential proclamation — in compact form.
- The Spirit falls before baptism (v.44), disrupting the normal order. God is not bound by sequence or ecclesiastical procedure. The church's role in this moment is not to initiate but to recognise and confirm what God has already done.
- Peter's conclusion — "Can anyone withhold water from these people?" (v.47) — is a rhetorical question with massive ecclesiological weight. The inclusion of the Gentiles is not a policy the church is granting; it is a reality the Spirit has already enacted.

#### Deeds
- Peter arrived at Cornelius's house having been transformed by a vision, not merely convinced by an argument. Transformation of prejudice requires revelation, not just information. Ask God to show you what you cannot yet see.
- Read Acts 10:34–43 slowly as a checklist of what the gospel actually is. How closely does what you share with others match Peter's outline here?
