# The Book of Acts
---

## Chapter 15
---

### 15.1 · The Council at Jerusalem (15:1–21)

**Key verse:** _"We believe it is through the grace of our Lord Jesus that we are saved, just as they are." — Acts 15:11_

#### Facts
- Certain men come down from Judea to Antioch teaching that Gentile believers must be circumcised according to the law of Moses to be saved.
- This creates sharp dispute, so Paul, Barnabas, and others are sent to Jerusalem to settle the issue with the apostles and elders.
- At the council, Peter reminds everyone how God chose him to preach to the Gentiles and gave them the Holy Spirit without distinction.
- James speaks up, quoting the prophets to show that God intended to rebuild David's fallen tent to include the Gentiles, and concludes they should not make it difficult for Gentiles turning to God.

#### Insights
- The Jerusalem Council preserves the core definition of the gospel: justification by grace through faith alone, without legalistic add-ons. Adding requirements to salvation distorts the work of Christ.
- Unity does not mean uniformity. The council respected the cultural differences of Gentile believers while maintaining structural and theological unity across the global church.

#### Deeds
- Check your own theological assumptions. Are there cultural traditions or secondary behaviors you unconsciously expect others to adopt before you consider them "true" Christians?

---

### 15.2 · The Council's Letter and Paul’s Second Mission (15:22–41)

**Key verse:** _"It seemed good to the Holy Spirit and to us not to burden you with anything beyond the following requirements..." — Acts 15:28_

#### Facts
- The council writes a letter to the Gentile believers, instructing them to abstain from food sacrificed to idols, from blood, from the meat of strangled animals, and from sexual immorality.
- Judas (Barsabbas) and Silas accompany Paul and Barnabas to deliver the letter to Antioch, where the believers read it and are encouraged.
- Sometime later, Paul suggests revisiting the churches, but Barnabas wants to take John Mark along, while Paul objects because Mark had previously abandoned them.
- The disagreement is so sharp that they part ways: Barnabas takes Mark to Cyprus, and Paul chooses Silas, traveling through Syria and Cilicia to strengthen the churches.

#### Insights
- The council's restrictions were not new requirements for salvation, but practical concessions to prevent unnecessary offense and allow Jewish and Gentile Christians to fellowship at the same dinner table.
- Even great leaders can experience sharp disagreements. God used the painful separation between Paul and Barnabas to double their missionary footprint, turning one mission team into two.

#### Deeds
- When working through interpersonal conflicts or disagreements in ministry, seek solutions that preserve the integrity of the gospel while allowing the work of God to move forward.
