# The Book of Acts
---

## Chapter 17
---

### 17.1 · Uproar in Thessalonica and the Noble Bereans (17:1–15)

**Key verse:** _"Now the Berean Jews were of more noble character than those in Thessalonica, for they received the message with great eagerness and examined the Scriptures every day to see if what Paul said was true." — Acts 17:11_

#### Facts
- Paul and Silas travel to Thessalonica and reason in the synagogue for three Sabbaths, explaining that the Messiah had to suffer and rise from the dead.
- Some Jews, God-fearing Greeks, and prominent women believe, but jealous Jews form a mob, ignite a riot, and attack the house of Jason, accusing the believers of defying Caesar's decrees.
- Paul and Silas escape by night to Berea, where the synagogue audience eagerly receives the message but cross-examines it daily against the Scriptures.
- When the Thessalonian agitators track them to Berea, Paul is sent to the coast and arrives in Athens.

#### Insights
- The Bereans provide the definitive biblical pattern for receiving teaching: they combined an open, eager heart with a critical, scriptural mind. Eagerness without examination leads to deception; examination without eagerness leads to skepticism.
- The gospel is fundamentally disruptive to worldly empires. The accusation that the apostles were "turning the world upside down" proves their message had massive societal implications.

#### Deeds
- Don't accept spiritual teaching at face value. Commit to reading the Scriptures daily to verify that what you are hearing aligns with God's word.

### 17.2 · Paul Preaches at the Areopagus in Athens (17:16–34)

**Key verse:** _"For as I walked around and looked carefully at your objects of worship, I even found an altar with this inscription: to an unknown god. So then, what you worship as something unknown I am going to proclaim to you." — Acts 17:23_

#### Facts
- While waiting in Athens, Paul is deeply distressed by the rampant idolatry in the city. He debates in the synagogue and marketplace with Epicurean and Stoic philosophers.
- They take him to the Areopagus (Mars Hill) to explain his "new teaching."
- Paul starts his sermon by referencing their altar "To an Unknown God," and preaches the Creator God who does not live in temples built by human hands.
- He quotes their own poets, calls for repentance, and mentions the resurrection, at which point some sneer, while a few others—including Dionysius and Damaris—believe.

#### Insights
- Paul contextualizes his approach without compromising his core message. He does not cite the Old Testament prophets to these Greek philosophers; instead, he uses their own cultural touchpoints to guide them to the true Creator.
- The resurrection is the non-negotiable line of demarcation. The intellectuals tolerated Paul's philosophy until he mentioned the resurrection of the dead, which demanded a personal choice.

#### Deeds
- Look closely at your local culture or workplace. What are the modern "altars to unknown gods" (longings for meaning, justice, peace)? Use those themes to start natural conversations about Jesus.
