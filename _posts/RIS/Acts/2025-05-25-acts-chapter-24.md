# The Book of Acts
---

## Chapter 24
---

### 24.1 · The Trial Before Felix (24:1–21)

**Key verse:** _"I strive always to keep my conscience clear before God and man." — Acts 24:16_

#### Facts
- Five days later, the high priest Ananias, some elders, and a lawyer named Tertullus travel to Caesarea to present their case against Paul before Governor Felix.
- Tertullus uses flattery and accuses Paul of being a troublemaker, a ringleader of the Nazarene sect, and an agitator stirring up riots among Jews worldwide.
- Paul defends himself calmly, pointing out there is no evidence for their claims, and states he was simply worshiping the God of his ancestors according to the Way, believing the law and the resurrection.
- He challenges his accusers to find any wrong in him, other than his statement regarding the resurrection of the dead.

#### Insights
- Truth does not need emotional flattery or false accusations to stand. In contrast to Tertullus's exaggerated rhetoric, Paul relies on simple facts, logic, and a clear conscience.
- The early Christians did not view themselves as an entirely separate religion, but as the true fulfillment of traditional Jewish hope rooted in the Scriptures.

#### Deeds
- Focus on maintaining a completely clear conscience before both God and the people around you. Let your lifestyle and honesty be your best defense when misunderstood.

### 24.2 · Paul’s Detention and Talks with Felix (24:22–27)

**Key verse:** _"As Paul talked about righteousness, self-control and the judgment to come, Felix was afraid and said, 'That's enough for now! You may leave.'" — Acts 24:25_

#### Facts
- Felix, who is well acquainted with the Way, adjourns the trial, ordering that Paul be kept under custody but given some freedom and allowed visits from his friends.
- Days later, Felix comes with his Jewish wife Drusilla and sends for Paul to hear him talk about faith in Christ Jesus.
- As Paul speaks directly about righteousness, self-control, and the judgment to come, Felix becomes frightened and dismisses him.
- Felix keeps Paul in prison for two years, hoping for a bribe; when he is succeeded by Porcius Festus, he leaves Paul in prison as a political favor to the Jews.

#### Insights
- True preaching addresses the conscience, not just the intellect. Paul did not flatter the powerful governor; he spoke about self-control and judgment, striking directly at Felix’s compromised life.
- Conviction without repentance leads to procrastination. Felix felt the weight of truth and became afraid, but instead of repenting, he put the decision off and looked for a financial bribe.

#### Deeds
- When God's word convicts you about a specific area of your life (like self-control or integrity), do not dismiss it or put off your response. Act on it immediately.
