# The Book of Acts
---

## Chapter 27
---

### 27.1 · The Voyage and the Storm at Sea (27:1–26)

**Key verse:** _"Last night an angel of the God to whom I belong and whom I serve stood beside me and said, 'Do not be afraid, Paul. You must stand trial before Caesar...'" — Acts 27:23–24_

#### Facts
- Paul and other prisoners are handed over to a centurion named Julius and board a ship sailing for Italy, accompanied by Luke and Aristarchus.
- Travel becomes slow and dangerous because of winter weather; Paul warns the officers that continuing the voyage will bring disaster, but the centurion follows the advice of the pilot and ship owner instead.
- A fierce northeaster hurricane (Euroclydon) strikes the ship, driving it out to sea; the crew throws cargo and ship tackle overboard to lighten the load, eventually losing all hope of survival.
- After days of darkness, Paul stands up, rebukes them gently for not listening to his warning, but tells them to take courage because an angel promised that no lives would be lost, though the ship will be destroyed.

#### Insights
- Godly wisdom is often ignored by worldly experts. The centurion trusted the commercial interests of the ship owner and the technical expertise of the pilot over Paul’s warning, resulting in catastrophe.
- In the midst of a collective crisis, a person connected to God becomes an anchor of hope. Paul’s courage was rooted in his identity: "the God to whom I belong and whom I serve."

#### Deeds
- When storms of crisis hit your family, workplace, or community, do you react with panic or with the steady confidence that comes from belonging to God? Be a voice of hope.

### 27.2 · The Shipwreck and Safe Landing (27:27–44)

**Key verse:** _"In this way everyone reached land safely." — Acts 27:44_

#### Facts
- On the fourteenth night, sailors sense land is near, take soundings, and drop four anchors from the stern to avoid hitting rocks.
- The sailors try to abandon ship using the lifeboat, but Paul alerts the centurion: "Unless these men stay with the ship, you cannot be saved," prompting the soldiers to cut the ropes.
- Before dawn, Paul urges everyone to eat for their survival, takes bread, gives thanks to God in front of them all, breaks it, and begins to eat, encouraging the 276 people on board.
- Daylight reveals a bay with a beach; they run the ship aground, where the bow jams fast and the stern is smashed by the surf. The centurion prevents the soldiers from killing the prisoners, and everyone reaches land safely on planks and pieces of wreckage.

#### Insights
- God's promises do not eliminate human responsibility. God promised all would survive, yet Paul clearly stated they must keep the crew on board and eat to stay alive. Divine sovereignty works through human means.
- Paul turns a chaotic survival scenario into an opportunity for worship, breaking bread and giving thanks publicly. Gratitude is possible even on a sinking ship.

#### Deeds
- Practice giving explicit thanks to God in public spaces, even during stressful or chaotic circumstances. Let your actions demonstrate your trust in His ultimate preservation.
