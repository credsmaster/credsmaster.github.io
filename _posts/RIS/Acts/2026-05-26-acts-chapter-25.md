# The Book of Acts
---

## Chapter 25
---

### 25.1 · Paul Appeals to Caesar (25:1–12)

**Key verse:** _"I am now standing before Caesar’s court, where I ought to be tried... I appeal to Caesar!" — Acts 25:10–11_

#### Facts
- Three days after arriving in the province, the new governor Festus goes to Jerusalem, where the chief priests ask him to transfer Paul back to Jerusalem, planning an ambush to kill him on the way.
- Festus refuses and invites them to Caesarea to bring charges; at the trial, the Jews make serious charges they cannot prove.
- Festus, wishing to do the Jews a favor, asks Paul if he is willing to go to Jerusalem to stand trial before him there.
- Paul refuses, stating he has done nothing wrong against the Jews, and exercises his right as a Roman citizen to appeal directly to Caesar. Festus confers with his council and grants the appeal.

#### Insights
- Institutional corruption can be highly persistent. Two years had passed, yet the religious leadership in Jerusalem still maintained the exact same murderous hatred and deceitful tactics.
- Paul's appeal to Caesar is a critical strategic pivot. It protects him from the Jewish ambush and legally guarantees that the Roman Empire must transport him directly to the capital.

#### Deeds
- When navigating complex structural or relational challenges, look for legal, wise, and strategic boundaries that preserve your safety and keep your mission on track.

### 25.2 · Festus Consults King Agrippa (25:13–27)

**Key verse:** _"But I found he had done nothing deserving of death, and because he himself appealed to the Emperor, I decided to send him." — Acts 25:25_

#### Facts
- A few days later, King Agrippa and Bernice arrive in Caesarea to pay their respects to Festus, who discusses Paul's unusual case with them.
- Festus admits to the king that Paul’s accusers brought no charges of the crimes he expected, but instead argued about their own religion and a dead man named Jesus whom Paul claims is alive.
- Agrippa expresses a desire to hear Paul himself, and Festus arranges it for the following day.
- The next day, Agrippa and Bernice enter the audience hall with great pomp, and Festus publicly states he has nothing definite to write to the Emperor about Paul, which is why he brought him out.

#### Insights
- To secular rulers like Festus, the core historical claim of Christianity—the resurrection of Jesus—looks like an obscure, irrelevant religious dispute. Yet it is the axle around which history turns.
- Human pomp and pageantry (Agrippa and Bernice's grand entrance) contrast sharply with the true spiritual authority held by the lone prisoner in chains standing before them.

#### Deeds
- Do not be intimidated by worldly status, wealth, or power. Keep your focus on the reality of the resurrection, which outlasts all human kingdoms.
