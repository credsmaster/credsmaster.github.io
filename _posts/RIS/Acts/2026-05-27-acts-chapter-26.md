# The Book of Acts
---

## Chapter 26
---

### 26.1 · Paul’s Defense Before Agrippa (26:1–23)

**Key verse:** _"So then, King Agrippa, I was not disobedient to the vision from heaven." — Acts 26:19_

#### Facts
- Agrippa permits Paul to speak, and Paul extends his hands, expressing gratitude to defend himself before a king well-versed in Jewish customs.
- Paul details his strict upbringing as a Pharisee and argues that he is on trial for the hope of God's promise to the ancestors.
- He recounts his conversion experience on the road to Damascus in greater detail, noting that Jesus spoke in Aramaic and told him: "It is hard for you to kick against the goads."
- Paul explains his divine commission to open the eyes of the Gentiles so they may turn from darkness to light and receive forgiveness through faith.

#### Insights
- "Kicking against the goads" is a vivid image of a stubborn ox resisting its master’s direction, only to cause itself more pain. Resisting God's promptings is ultimately futile and self-destructive.
- Paul redefines his mission not as a radical departure from Israel's faith, but as direct obedience to the heavenly vision that fulfills the prophets and Moses.

#### Deeds
- Reflect on whether you are "kicking against the goads" in any area where God has been persistently nudging or correcting you. Choose obedience over resistance.

### 26.2 · The Reaction of Festus and Agrippa (26:24–32)

**Key verse:** _"Short time or long—I pray to God that not only you but all who are listening to me today may become what I am, except for these chains." — Acts 26:29_

#### Facts
- As Paul speaks, Festus interrupts loudly, shouting, "You are out of your mind, Paul! Your great learning is driving you insane."
- Paul calmly replies that he is speaking rational truth and appeals to King Agrippa, noting that these historic events were not done in a hidden corner.
- Paul asks Agrippa if he believes the prophets; Agrippa retorts, "Do you think that in such a short time you can persuade me to be a Christian?"
- Paul expresses his deep desire that everyone listening would become like him, minus the chains. The leaders private conclude that Paul is completely innocent and could have been freed had he not appealed to Caesar.

#### Insights
- To a purely materialistic or cynical mindset, absolute commitment to Christ looks like insanity. Festus saw deep learning but concluded it was madness.
- Agrippa tries to deflect Paul’s sharp personal question with a sarcastic defense mechanism. True faith demands a personal verdict, which powerful people often avoid to protect their status.

#### Deeds
- When sharing your faith, be prepared for others to dismiss you or call you extreme. Respond with the same calm, dignified, and loving confidence that Paul displayed.
