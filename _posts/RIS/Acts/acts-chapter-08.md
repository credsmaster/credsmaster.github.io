# The Book of Acts
---

## Chapter 8
---

### 8.1 · The Church Scattered — Philip in Samaria (8:1–25)

**Key verse:** _"Those who had been scattered preached the word wherever they went." — Acts 8:4_

#### Facts
- A great persecution breaks out in Jerusalem, led by Saul; believers scatter throughout Judea and Samaria (fulfilling Acts 1:8), though the apostles remain in Jerusalem.
- Philip goes to Samaria and proclaims the Messiah there with signs and great joy; many believe and are baptised.
- Simon the sorcerer, who had amazed the region with his magic, also believes and follows Philip.
- Peter and John come from Jerusalem; they pray and lay hands on the Samaritans, who receive the Holy Spirit.
- Simon offers money to receive this power; Peter rebukes him sharply for thinking the gift of God can be purchased.

#### Insights
- Persecution is the mechanism God uses to break Jerusalem's monopoly on the gospel. What looks like a disaster is, from a mission perspective, a diaspora — seeds scattered to new soil.
- Samaritans were half-Jewish outcasts, despised by Judean Jews. Philip crossing into Samaria is not a minor boundary — it is a significant cultural and theological step toward "the ends of the earth."
- Simon Magus's error (simony — buying spiritual office or power) is not just about money. It reflects a fundamentally transactional view of God: power as a commodity you can own. Peter's rebuke is a perennial warning to every generation.

#### Deeds
- Has a difficulty in your life ever turned out to be the means of spreading something good you would not have spread otherwise? Name it and thank God.
- Examine your own posture toward spiritual gifts or ministry: do you desire them to serve others, or to enhance your status?

### 8.2 · Philip and the Ethiopian Eunuch (8:26–40)

**Key verse:** _"Do you understand what you are reading?" Philip asked. "How can I," he said, "unless someone guides me?" — Acts 8:30–31_

#### Facts
- An angel directs Philip to leave Samaria and go south on the desert road toward Gaza.
- He encounters an Ethiopian eunuch — a court official of the queen — sitting in his chariot reading Isaiah 53.
- The Spirit prompts Philip to approach; the eunuch is reading about a sheep led to slaughter and asks who the passage refers to.
- Philip explains that it refers to Jesus; the eunuch believes and asks to be baptised immediately at a pool of water.
- After the baptism, Philip is suddenly taken away by the Spirit; the eunuch continues his journey rejoicing.

#### Insights
- The Ethiopian eunuch is a double outsider — a foreigner and a eunuch, both groups with restricted access to the Jerusalem temple (Deut 23:1). Yet he is seeking God earnestly, reading Israel's Scripture in a chariot in the desert. God meets genuine seekers in unexpected places.
- Isaiah 53, the Suffering Servant passage, is precisely the text that opens a natural bridge to Jesus. God had been preparing this man's heart before Philip arrived.
- Philip's obedience is specific and immediate — he runs (v.30). The Spirit's prompting deserves a prompt response, not a committee meeting.

#### Deeds
- Who in your life is an "Ethiopian eunuch" — an outsider sincerely seeking, but needing someone to guide them? Ask God to open your eyes to that person.
- Sit with Isaiah 52:13–53:12 this week. Read it as Philip would have explained it. What fresh light does it cast on Jesus?
