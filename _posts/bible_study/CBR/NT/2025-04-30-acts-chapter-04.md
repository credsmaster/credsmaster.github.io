# The Book of Acts
---

## Chapter 4
---

### 4.1 · Peter and John Before the Sanhedrin (4:1–22)

**Key verse:** _"Salvation is found in no one else, for there is no other name under heaven given to mankind by which we must be saved." — Acts 4:12_

#### Facts
- The priests, the captain of the temple guard, and the Sadducees arrest Peter and John, disturbed by their proclamation of resurrection in Jesus.
- Before the Sanhedrin — the same body that tried Jesus — Peter, filled with the Holy Spirit, delivers a bold defence centred on the name of Jesus.
- The rulers note that Peter and John are "unschooled, ordinary men" yet are astonished by their boldness, recognising they had been with Jesus.
- Unable to deny the healed man standing before them, the authorities threaten the apostles and release them.

#### Insights
- Acts 4:12 is not a philosophical claim made in a seminar room — it is a declaration made under arrest, before hostile authorities, at personal cost. Exclusivity here is courageous, not casual.
- "They had been with Jesus" (v.13) is perhaps the most telling commentary on what produces boldness in witness. Formation precedes proclamation.
- The Sanhedrin cannot deny the miracle (v.16) but refuse its implications. This is a pattern throughout Acts and human history: evidence alone does not produce faith; the heart must be willing.

#### Deeds
- When facing opposition, does your witness become bolder or quieter? Ask God for the Spirit's boldness that the apostles demonstrated.
- Invest in "being with Jesus" — unhurried time in prayer and Scripture — and watch how it changes your capacity to speak of him.

### 4.2 · The Believers' Prayer and Community Life (4:23–37)

**Key verse:** _"After they prayed, the place where they were meeting was shaken. And they were all filled with the Holy Spirit and spoke the word of God boldly." — Acts 4:31_

#### Facts
- Released, Peter and John return to the believers and report everything. The community responds not with fear or a strategy meeting, but with prayer.
- They quote Psalm 2 and frame their persecution within God's sovereign plan — what Herod and Pilate did to Jesus is being replicated, and God is still sovereign.
- The prayer is answered immediately: the place shakes, all are filled with the Spirit, and they speak boldly.
- Barnabas is introduced — he sells a field and brings the proceeds to the apostles, exemplifying the generosity characterising the community.

#### Insights
- The community's first response to threat is worship, not worry — they pray Psalm 2, a text about God's sovereign rule over hostile nations. Their theology determines their emotional posture.
- The prayer does not ask for safety but for boldness and signs. This is a radically different prayer instinct from most modern believers under pressure.
- Barnabas (Son of Encouragement) makes his first appearance through an act of sacrificial generosity. Character and action are introduced together — this is who he is.

#### Deeds
- When your community faces opposition or difficulty, does it pray? And if so, what does it ask for?
- Consider Barnabas's posture: he is not named an apostle here, but he is named. Faithful generosity is seen and remembered.
