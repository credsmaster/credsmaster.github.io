# Part 1 — The Book of Signs (Chapters 1–12)

---

## Chapter 1

---

### 1.1 · The Word Made Flesh (1:1–18)

**Key verse:** _In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God." — John 1:1_

#### Facts
- John opens not with a birth narrative but with a cosmic prologue echoing Genesis: "In the beginning." 
- The Word (Logos) is identified as the agent of all creation — nothing was made without him.
- The Word became flesh and "dwelt among us" (tabernacled), and the disciples beheld his glory.
 

#### Insights
- John's prologue is a theological declaration before a single event is narrated — he wants us to know who Jesus is before we see what he does.
- "The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it" — this tension between light and darkness runs through the entire gospel.
- Grace and truth came through Jesus Christ; the Law came through Moses. John draws a deliberate contrast, not a contradiction.
- 


#### Deeds
- Meditate on what it means that God chose to dwell among people — not speak from a distance, but come near.
- Ask yourself: Do I relate to Jesus primarily as a teacher, a miracle-worker, or as the eternal Word of God?

### 1.2 · Testimony of John the Baptist (1:19–34)

**Key verse:** _Write your key verse here_

#### Facts
- Jewish leaders send priests and Levites from Jerusalem to ask John directly: "Who are you?"
John denies being the Messiah, Elijah, or the Prophet — he identifies himself only as "a voice crying in the wilderness."
- John sees the Spirit descend on Jesus like a dove and declares him "the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world."
- 

#### Insights
- John the Baptist's self-definition is striking: he refuses every title and instead points entirely away from himself. There is a lesson in godly ambition here.
- "Lamb of God" would have carried immediate weight for a Jewish audience — the Passover lamb, the daily temple sacrifice. John is announcing that the whole sacrificial system finds its fulfilment in one person.
- The Spirit remaining on Jesus (v.33) distinguishes him from the prophets, on whom the Spirit came and went.
- 


#### Deeds
- Reflect: Is there any area of ministry or life where I am drawing attention to myself rather than to Christ?
- Consider the courage it took John to say plainly, "I am not the Christ" when people were ready to make him one.

### 1.3 · First Disciples Called — Andrew, Peter, Philip, Nathanael (1:35–51)

#### Facts
- Andrew and an unnamed disciple (likely John) follow Jesus after John the Baptist points him out.
- Andrew immediately finds his brother Simon and brings him to Jesus — the first act of personal evangelism in this gospel.
- Philip is called directly by Jesus; he then finds Nathanael, who is sceptical ("Can anything good come out of Nazareth?").
- Jesus tells Nathanael he saw him under the fig tree before Philip called him, and Nathanael's scepticism turns to confession.
- 


#### Insights
- The pattern of "come and see" is the gospel's invitation — not "accept a proposition" but "come, encounter, discover."
- Andrew's first instinct after meeting Jesus is to find his brother. Genuine encounter with Christ naturally overflows into witness.
- Jesus' knowledge of Nathanael under the fig tree signals his divine omniscience, foreshadowing later revelations of his knowledge of people's hearts.
- 

#### Deeds
- Who is your "Simon" — the person closest to you whom you could bring to Jesus?
- Sit with the phrase "Come and see" this week. Is your own faith an invitation to others, or is it private?
