# Part 1 — The Book of Signs (Chapters 1–12)

---

## Chapter 3

---

### 3.1 · Nicodemus — Born Again (3:1–15)


#### Facts
- Nicodemus is a Pharisee and a ruler of the Jews — he comes to Jesus at night.
- Jesus tells him that no one can see the kingdom of God unless they are born "from above" (the Greek anothen means both "again" and "from above").
- Nicodemus misunderstands physically; Jesus clarifies the new birth is of water and Spirit.
- 
- 

#### Insights
- Nicodemus comes at night — possibly for secrecy, possibly a symbol of his spiritual state. John's gospel uses light and darkness as spiritual categories throughout.
- That Jesus says this to a Pharisee and teacher of Israel is pointed: religious knowledge and moral standing are not enough. A complete new beginning is required.
- The reference to Moses lifting up the serpent (Numbers 21) foreshadows the crucifixion — the Son of Man must be "lifted up" so that those who look to him in faith may live.
- 
- 

#### Deeds
- Examine whether your faith is based on religious performance and knowledge, or on a genuine new birth by the Spirit.
- Pray through what it means practically to be "born of the Spirit" — not a one-time event but an ongoing life.
-

### 3.2 · God So Loved the World (3:16–21)

Key verse: "For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life." — John 3:16

#### Facts
- "The world" (kosmos) here is the object of God's love — the same world John elsewhere says is hostile to God. The scope of the love is universal.
- God's purpose in sending the Son is not condemnation but salvation.
- Those who do not believe are already condemned; those who do evil hate the light and avoid it.
- 

#### Insights
- John 3:16 is rightly called the gospel in miniature, but it is easily over-familiared. Sit with the word "gave" — this is sacrifice, not loan.
- The passage shifts the locus of judgment: it is not that God actively condemns, but that people condemn themselves by refusing the light.
- "Eternal life" in John's gospel is not merely endless life — it is a quality of life, the life of the age to come, begun now.
- 


#### Deeds
- Memorise John 3:16–17 as a pair — the "not to condemn" of v.17 is as important as the love of v.16.
- Reflect on whether you "come to the light" — are you open before God, or do you avoid the exposure that honest prayer and Scripture reading brings?

### 3.3 · John the Baptist Decreases (3:22–36)

**Key verse:** _Write your key verse here_

#### Facts
- Jesus and his disciples are also baptising, and John's disciples are troubled that people are going to Jesus instead of John.
- John responds with the parable of the bridegroom — he is only the friend of the bridegroom, not the groom himself.
- The passage ends with a sharp contrast: the one who comes from above is above all; the one who rejects the Son will not see life.
- 

#### Insights
- John's contentment in decreasing is one of the most counter-cultural postures in the New Testament. Ministry that points to itself rather than to Christ has inverted its purpose.
- The "friend of the bridegroom" image: John's joy is complete precisely because he has heard the bridegroom's voice and the wedding is happening. His diminishment is not loss — it is fulfilment.
- The passage ends with stark eschatology: the wrath of God remains on those who reject the Son. John's gospel is gentle but never soft on the stakes.
- 

#### Deeds
- Where in your life do you need to consciously decrease so that Christ can increase?
- Reflect on whether your joy is tied to your own visibility or to the advance of God's kingdom.
