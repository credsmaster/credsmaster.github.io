# Part 1 — The Book of Signs (Chapters 1–12)

---

## Chapter 5

---

### 5.1 · Healing at Bethesda — Third Sign (5:1–15)

**Key verse:** _Write your key verse here_

#### Facts
- At the pool of Bethesda in Jerusalem, there is a man who has been an invalid for 38 years.
- Jesus asks him if he wants to be healed — the man's response is not "yes" but an excuse about having no one to help him into the pool.
- Jesus heals him with a word: "Get up, take up your bed, and walk." This happens on the Sabbath.
- The healed man later tells the Jewish leaders it was Jesus who healed him.
- 


#### Insights
- "Do you want to be healed?" is not a cruel question — it is a penetrating one. Long illness can become an identity. Jesus addresses the will before addressing the body.
- The man never expresses faith, never asks Jesus for help, never even knows who Jesus is until later. This sign is entirely on Jesus' initiative — grace without precondition.
- The Sabbath controversy that follows is not incidental. John is building toward the claim that Jesus is Lord of the Sabbath and equal to the Father.
- 

#### Deeds
- Ask honestly: Is there any area of brokenness in your life that you have stopped wanting to be healed of?
- Sit with the unconditional grace of this healing — Jesus moved toward this man without being asked. Let that shape your understanding of how God moves toward you.

### 5.2 · Jesus' Authority as the Son (5:16–30)

**Key verse:** _Truly, truly, I say to you, whoever hears my word and believes him who sent me has eternal life." — John 5:24_

#### Facts
- The Jewish leaders persecute Jesus for healing on the Sabbath. His response — "My Father is working until now, and I am working" — makes them more intent on killing him, as he was "making himself equal with God."
- Jesus explains that the Son does only what he sees the Father doing; the Father and Son work in complete unity.
- Jesus claims authority to give life and to judge — both divine prerogatives — and says the hour is coming when the dead will hear the voice of the Son of God and live.
- 

#### Insights
- This is one of the highest Christological passages in John. Jesus does not back away from the charge of making himself equal with God — he elaborates on it.
- The relationship between Father and Son is one of perfect love and perfect unity: "the Father loves the Son and shows him all that he himself is doing."
- Judgment and life-giving are the same act: to hear the voice of the Son and believe is to pass from death to life. The judgment is not an additional event — it is the present reality of how one responds to Jesus.
- 

#### Deeds
- Read this passage slowly and let the scale of who Jesus claims to be land afresh. Worship is the appropriate response.
- Consider: What does it mean for daily life that the one you are following has authority over life, death, and judgment?

### 5.3 · Witnesses to Jesus (5:31–47)

**Key verse:** _You search the Scriptures because you think that in them you have eternal life; and it is they that bear witness about me." — John 5:39_

#### Facts
- Jesus names four witnesses to his identity: John the Baptist, his own works, the Father, and the Scriptures.
- He charges the religious leaders with refusing to come to him even though Moses — whom they claim to follow — wrote about him.
- They receive honour from one another rather than seeking the honour that comes from God.
- 


#### Insights
- The Scriptures are not an end in themselves — they are witnesses pointing to Christ. It is possible to study the Bible intensively and entirely miss what it is about.
- The diagnosis of the leaders' unbelief is not intellectual but moral and social: they love human approval more than God's approval (v.44). This is a recurring theme in John.
- Moses as a witness against them is a sharp irony — the very foundation of their identity testifies against their rejection of Jesus.
- 


#### Deeds
- Examine your own Bible reading: Are you reading to encounter the Christ the Scriptures point to, or to accumulate knowledge?
- Ask God to free you from the approval of others as a filter through which you make spiritual decisions.
