# Part 1 — The Book of Signs (Chapters 1–12)

---

## Chapter 6

---

### 6.1 · Feeding the Five Thousand — Fourth Sign (6:1–15)

**Key verse:** _There is a boy here who has five barley loaves and two fish, but what are they for so many?" — John 6:9_

#### Facts
- A large crowd follows Jesus because of his signs. He tests Philip: "Where are we to buy bread for these people to eat?"
- Andrew finds a boy with five barley loaves and two fish — a meagre resource.
- Jesus gives thanks, distributes the food, and all eat until satisfied; twelve baskets of fragments are gathered.
- The crowd wants to make him king by force; Jesus withdraws.
- 

#### Insights
- Jesus tests Philip, but John tells us "he himself knew what he would do" (v.6). The test is for Philip's benefit, not for Jesus' information. God often lets us feel the impossibility before he acts.
- Barley loaves were the food of the poor — this is not a wealthy crowd. The sign meets real, physical need.
- The gathering of twelve baskets of leftovers echoes Elisha's feeding miracle (2 Kings 4), but on a vastly greater scale. Jesus is not another prophet — he exceeds them all.
- The crowd's response — make him king — misunderstands the sign. They want provision without the Person.
- 


#### Deeds
- What "five loaves and two fish" do you have — small, inadequate resources — that you need to place in Jesus' hands rather than hoarding or dismissing?
- Beware of seeking Jesus primarily for what he provides. The sign points beyond itself.

### 6.2 · Walking on Water — Fifth Sign (6:16–24)

**Key verse:** _It is I; do not be afraid." — John 6:20_

#### Facts
- The disciples set out by boat at night without Jesus; a strong wind rises and the sea becomes rough.
- After rowing three or four miles, they see Jesus walking on the sea and are terrified.
- Jesus says "It is I; do not be afraid" — the Greek is Egō eimi, "I AM."
Immediately the boat reaches land.
- 


#### Insights
- "I AM" (Egō eimi) echoes the divine name revealed to Moses. John intends the reader to hear it as more than identification — it is a divine self-disclosure.
- The disciples are in the storm not because of disobedience but because Jesus sent them ahead. Difficulty does not mean absence of God or departure from his will.
- "Immediately the boat was at the land" — John records no gradual arrival. The presence of Jesus resolves the crisis completely and suddenly.
- 


#### Deeds
- In the storms you are currently in, hear Jesus say Egō eimi — "I AM is here." Let that be the source of your steadiness.
- Reflect: Do you tend to interpret difficulty as God's absence or discipline, rather than as a moment of imminent divine arrival?

### 6.3 · Bread of Life Discourse (6:25–59)

**Key verse:** _I am the bread of life; whoever comes to me shall not hunger, and whoever believes in me shall never thirst." — John 6:35_

#### Facts
- The crowd finds Jesus in Capernaum and he confronts their motive: they seek him because they ate the bread, not because they understood the sign.
- Jesus contrasts perishable food with "the food that endures to eternal life." The work of God is simply to believe in the one he has sent.
- Jesus identifies himself as the true bread from heaven, greater than the manna Moses gave — that bread left people still dying; this bread gives life to the world.
He speaks of eating his flesh and drinking his blood, which causes enormous offence.
- 
- 

#### Insights
- "What must we do to do the works of God?" — the crowd thinks in terms of actions. Jesus reframes: the work is believing, not performing. This dismantles every religion of achievement.
- The manna comparison is decisive: Israel's fathers ate manna and died. Jesus offers bread that means you "will not die." He is not offering a better version of what went before — he is offering something categorically new.
- The eating and drinking language anticipates the Eucharist but also functions as a metaphor for total, personal reception of Jesus — not merely intellectual assent but inward incorporation.
- 
- 

#### Deeds
- Ask yourself honestly: Are you seeking Jesus himself, or the benefits Jesus provides?
- Sit with John 6:35 as a personal promise. Bring your hungers and thirsts to him specifically.

### 6.4 · Many Disciples Turn Back; Peter's Confession (6:60–71)

**Key verse:** _Write your key verse here_

#### Facts
- Many of Jesus' disciples say "This is a hard saying; who can listen to it?" and turn back from following him.
- Jesus asks the Twelve: "Do you want to go away as well?"
- Peter answers on their behalf with one of the great confessions of the gospel.
Jesus acknowledges that even among the Twelve, one is "a devil" — referring to Judas.
- 


#### Insights
- John records a mass departure of disciples here — not enemies, but followers who had been with Jesus. The gospel's demands are not always comfortable, and following is always a choice renewed.
- Peter's confession is not triumphant certainty but faithful loyalty: "to whom shall we go?" There is nowhere else. This is the faith of someone who has counted the alternatives and found them empty.
- The mention of Judas at this moment is sobering — proximity to Jesus, even being chosen by him, is no guarantee of a final faithful response.
- 


#### Deeds
- Peter's answer is a good one for seasons of doubt: not "I have no questions" but "there is nowhere else to go." Let that honest loyalty carry you through difficulty.
- Examine your own discipleship: Are you following Jesus because of what he gives, and liable to leave when it becomes costly? Or have you settled somewhere deeper?
