# The Book of Acts
---

## Chapter 7
---

### 7.1 · Stephen's Speech to the Sanhedrin (7:1–53)

**Key verse:** _"You stiff-necked people! Your hearts and ears are still uncircumcised. You are just like your ancestors: you always resist the Holy Spirit!" — Acts 7:51_

#### Facts
- Stephen delivers the longest speech in Acts, retelling Israel's history from Abraham through Joseph, Moses, the wilderness period, the tabernacle, and Solomon's temple.
- His argument builds to a sharp point: throughout their history, Israel repeatedly rejected the messengers God sent — Joseph by his brothers, Moses twice by the people.
- Stephen concludes by accusing the council of betraying and murdering Jesus, the Righteous One, just as their ancestors murdered the prophets.
- The council is furious; Stephen, full of the Holy Spirit, sees a vision of heaven opened and Jesus standing at the right hand of God.

#### Insights
- Stephen does not mount a defence — he mounts an indictment. His retelling of Israel's history is a masterclass in showing that rejection of God's chosen deliverers is the pattern, not the exception. Jesus is the climax of a long story of rejection.
- The vision of Jesus "standing" (v.56) rather than seated is theologically significant — it has been read as Jesus rising to receive his faithful martyr, or as a witness standing to give testimony on Stephen's behalf.
- Stephen's speech is also an implicit argument that God has never been confined to a building. He was with Abraham in Mesopotamia, Joseph in Egypt, Moses in Midian. The temple is not where God is locked in.

#### Insights
- Stephen's use of Scripture is probing and offensive in the best sense — it exposes the contradiction between what the people profess to honour (Moses, the prophets) and what they actually do (reject their message).

#### Deeds
- How well do you know the narrative of Scripture from Genesis onward? Stephen's power comes partly from knowing the whole story. Begin or continue reading the Old Testament as a unified account.
- Ask yourself honestly: in what ways might you be "resisting the Holy Spirit" — refusing to hear what God has been persistently saying?

### 7.2 · The Stoning of Stephen (7:54–60)

**Key verse:** _"Lord, do not hold this sin against them." — Acts 7:60_

#### Facts
- The council is enraged; they drag Stephen out of the city and stone him. The witnesses lay their coats at the feet of a young man named Saul.
- As he is being stoned, Stephen prays twice — first "Lord Jesus, receive my spirit," then "Lord, do not hold this sin against them."
- He falls asleep — the Greek word for death used here is *koimaomai*, to sleep.

#### Insights
- Stephen's two final prayers are a direct echo of Jesus's words from the cross (Luke 23:34, 46). He dies as Jesus died. This is not coincidence — it is the Spirit forming Christ in his followers to the end.
- Saul is introduced as a consenting bystander. His coat-holding is a detail that Luke, the author, will not let us forget. The road to Damascus has its prologue here.
- The word "sleep" for death (v.60) carries early Christian confidence: death is not annihilation but a resting in God, pending resurrection. How you speak of death says much about what you believe.

#### Deeds
- Pray through Stephen's final words today. Could you pray for those who harm or oppose you with the same specificity and grace?
- Note that Stephen was a table-server who became a martyr. Faithfulness in small things opens doors to the largest tests of character.
