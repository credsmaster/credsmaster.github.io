# The Book of Acts
---

## Chapter 9
---

### 9.1 · Saul's Conversion (9:1–19a)

**Key verse:** _"Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me?" — Acts 9:4_

#### Facts
- Saul, still threatening the disciples, obtains letters from the high priest to arrest believers in Damascus.
- On the road, a blinding light knocks him down; a voice identifies itself as Jesus, asking why Saul is persecuting him.
- Saul is led blind to Damascus, where for three days he neither eats nor drinks.
- God directs a disciple named Ananias to go to Saul. Ananias is afraid — he knows Saul's reputation — but obeys.
- Ananias lays hands on him, Saul's sight is restored, he is filled with the Holy Spirit and baptised.

#### Insights
- Jesus's question — "Why do you persecute me?" — reveals the deep solidarity between Christ and his people. To persecute the church is to persecute Jesus himself. The body metaphor in Paul's later letters is not metaphor; it is lived experience from the Damascus road.
- Saul's three days of blindness, fasting, and prayer echo baptismal and resurrection imagery — a death and rebirth. He who came to arrest emerges arrested.
- Ananias is one of the unsung heroes of Acts. He receives a dangerous command, voices his honest fear, and obeys anyway. His obedience changes the trajectory of the entire Gentile mission.

#### Deeds
- God called the most dangerous opponent of the church to be its greatest missionary. Who in your life seems the most unlikely candidate for faith? Pray for them specifically this week.
- Reflect on Ananias's pattern: honest fear + full obedience. This is not blind courage but faith that acts despite knowing the cost.

### 9.2 · Saul Preaches in Damascus and Jerusalem (9:19b–31)

**Key verse:** _"Yet Saul grew more and more powerful and baffled the Jews living in Damascus by proving that Jesus is the Messiah." — Acts 9:22_

#### Facts
- Saul immediately begins preaching in the Damascus synagogues, to the astonishment of all who knew his previous mission.
- His preaching grows in power; the Jewish leaders in Damascus plot to kill him, so the disciples lower him through a wall in a basket at night to escape.
- In Jerusalem, Barnabas vouches for Saul to the still-suspicious apostles. Saul debates boldly with Greek-speaking Jews, who also plot to kill him.
- The brothers send him to Caesarea and then to Tarsus; the church throughout Judea, Galilee, and Samaria then enjoys a period of peace and growth.

#### Insights
- "Baffled" (v.22) — the Greek means to confuse or prove incapable of answering. Saul's arguments were not merely sincere but intellectually rigorous. Faith and robust reasoning are not opponents.
- Barnabas's advocacy for Saul (v.27) is another act of courageous generosity from the "Son of Encouragement." Without it, Saul may have remained a suspicious outsider to the Jerusalem church. The willingness to vouch for people on the margins is ministry.
- The church's growth follows Saul's departure (v.31). God is not tied to one person's gifting. Sometimes peace comes when a catalytic personality steps back.

#### Deeds
- Is there someone in your community who needs a "Barnabas" — someone to vouch for them, open a door, or give them a fair hearing?
- Saul went to Tarsus after Jerusalem — a period of obscurity before the great missionary journeys. Are you willing to wait in your "Tarsus" rather than forcing your way onto a larger stage?

### 9.3 · Aeneas and Dorcas (9:32–43)

**Key verse:** _"Tabitha, get up." She opened her eyes, and seeing Peter she sat up." — Acts 9:40_

#### Facts
- Peter travels through the region and comes to Lydda, where he heals Aeneas — paralysed for eight years — who rises immediately.
- All who live in Lydda and Sharon see him and turn to the Lord.
- In nearby Joppa, a disciple named Tabitha (Dorcas) — known for good works and charity — dies. The believers urgently summon Peter.
- Peter sends everyone out, kneels and prays, and calls her to rise. She opens her eyes, and Peter presents her alive to the community.
- Many believe in Joppa as a result; Peter stays there with Simon the tanner.

#### Insights
- Dorcas is described before the miracle by her character: "always doing good and helping the poor" (v.36). Her reputation is built on a life of practical compassion. The miracle confirms what her life had already demonstrated.
- The widows' grief at Dorcas's death (v.39) — showing Peter the garments she made — is a portrait of love expressed in labour. Small acts of service create communities of profound attachment.
- Peter's raising of Dorcas echoes Jesus raising Jairus's daughter (Mark 5:41, "Talitha kum" / "Tabitha kum"). The parallel is not accidental — the risen Jesus continues his ministry through his Spirit-filled people.

#### Deeds
- What would your community say if asked to describe you? Would they point primarily to spiritual giftings, or to a life of consistent, practical love?
- The widows grieved Dorcas because of what her hands had done. Ask God to make your life one that leaves behind a legacy of service, not just words.
